The 40° Rule

Summary of feedback received and research on visual ergonomics for desktop web design

The feedback we received

"I always teach about the 40° horizontal scope that the eye sees comfortably. Somewhere between a 13" and 17" screen — depending on how close or far we are from the screen — we exceed that scope. When that happens, I expect the site to have empty margins to focus reading, especially when high user concentration is needed: shop, about, team, services."

The principle

The human eye has a natural cone of comfortable focus spanning roughly 40° horizontally. Within this cone, the eye reads without strain. Beyond it, the user must physically move their eyes or head — which adds cognitive load, especially during focused tasks like reading, browsing services, or making booking decisions.

Why screen size matters

The 40° threshold is about the relationship between screen size and viewing distance, not screen size alone. At a typical laptop distance of 50–70cm:

  • A 13" screen generally fits within the comfortable cone
  • A 17" screen (or any large monitor at close range) starts to exceed it — particularly at the corners

Full-width layouts on larger screens force unnecessary eye travel across the full canvas.

Design recommendations

Constrain the text and content column to roughly 650–800px, centered, with intentional empty margins. The margins are not wasted space — they are an ergonomic decision that keeps reading within the comfortable cone.

Section typeRecommended approach
Hero / brand momentFull width — fine
Services / pricingCentered column, ~700px max
About / teamCentered column, ~700px max
Booking / checkout flowCentered, narrower (~560px)
Gallery / portfolioFull width — fine

Full-width layouts can still be correct

The rule applies to reading and decision-making contexts, not to visual or brand moments. A wide layout with a constrained text column is exactly the right pattern — wide canvas, narrow content zone.

A good example: the Erella Hair Studio hero uses a full-width split layout, but the text block on the left is only ~400px wide — well within the cone. The right side is a single image, handled by peripheral vision with no reading required. This is the correct approach.

  • Wide visual composition → full width is valid
  • Text block within that composition → already constrained and comfortable
  • The problem only arises when text itself spans the full width on large screens
